The Complete Guide to Moving to Houston in 2026

July 15, 2026  ·  7 min read

← Back to Home

Houston is the fourth-largest city in the United States, and for good reason — it offers one of the most compelling combinations of economic opportunity, cultural diversity, and affordability of any major American metro. No state income tax, a booming job market, and a food scene that rivals any coastal city make it an increasingly popular destination for people relocating from high-cost states like California, New York, and Illinois.

But moving to Houston without preparation is a mistake. The city is enormous, the traffic is real, and the summers are brutal. Here is everything you need to know before you make the move.

Best Neighborhoods in Houston

Montrose is Houston's most eclectic and walkable neighborhood — a dense urban pocket of art galleries, independent restaurants, coffee shops, and live music venues. It attracts artists, young professionals, and longtime Houstonians who want character over square footage. If you want a neighborhood that feels like a city, Montrose delivers.

The Heights sits just north of downtown and has transformed over the past decade into one of Houston's most sought-after addresses. Victorian bungalows line tree-canopied streets, and White Oak Bayou provides a green trail corridor through the neighborhood. Expect competitive real estate, but the quality of life justifies it.

Midtown sits between downtown and Montrose and is the go-to choice for recent graduates and young professionals who want to be close to the action without Montrose rents. The light rail runs through it, making car-free commutes to downtown legitimately possible — a rarity in Houston.

Sugar Land, southwest of the city proper, is Houston's most family-friendly suburb. Top-rated schools, master-planned communities, and a diverse population make it particularly appealing for families relocating from other regions. The commute to downtown runs 30–45 minutes on a good day.

Cost of Living

Houston is significantly more affordable than comparable metros. The median home price sits around $310,000 — less than half of what the same square footage costs in Los Angeles or Seattle. Renters find similar relief: a two-bedroom apartment in a desirable neighborhood averages $1,400–$1,800 per month. Combined with Texas's zero state income tax, take-home pay goes considerably further here than in high-tax states.

Utilities are the asterisk. Houston summers are long and hot, and air conditioning bills from June through September can run $200–$350 per month for a typical apartment. Factor that in when budgeting.

Job Market

Houston's economy is anchored by energy — it is home to more than 4,600 energy-related firms — but the city has diversified aggressively into healthcare, aerospace, manufacturing, and technology. The Texas Medical Center is the world's largest medical complex and a major employer. NASA's Johnson Space Center is based here. And tech companies including Amazon, Google, and Microsoft have expanded Houston operations significantly in recent years.

Weather: The Honest Answer

Houston summers are no joke. From late May through mid-October, temperatures routinely hit 95–100°F with humidity that makes it feel 10 degrees hotter. Outdoor activities shift to early morning or evening during these months. Winters, by contrast, are mild — January averages 53°F, and snow is rare enough to be a local event.

Hurricane season runs June through November. Houston took devastating hits from Harvey in 2017, and flooding remains a real risk in low-lying neighborhoods. Research the flood history of any property before buying or renting, and consider flood insurance even where it is not required.

Things To Do

Houston's cultural footprint is larger than its national reputation suggests. The Museum District packs 19 museums into a single walkable corridor. The Houston Livestock Show and Rodeo is the world's largest, drawing 2.5 million visitors each February and March. The food scene is genuinely world-class, driven by the city's remarkable diversity — Vietnamese, Nigerian, Mexican, and Indian cuisines each have entire neighborhoods dedicated to them.

If you are comparing Houston to other Texas cities, Austin offers a smaller, more walkable footprint with a stronger music identity, but at significantly higher cost. Houston wins on value, scale, and economic opportunity.

Before You Move

Get a car. Houston is not a walking city outside of a handful of neighborhoods, and the bus system is limited. Research flood maps for any property you consider. Visit in February or March — not July — to get your best impression of the city before committing. And get ready to discover that Houston's food scene may be the best-kept secret in American dining.
